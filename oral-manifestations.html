Habits & behaviors

Oral Manifestations

Many common habits — thumb sucking, nail biting, hair twirling — may seem harmless, but when they persist they can have real consequences for orofacial development and function.

Understanding oral habits

Repetitive oral and self-soothing habits are a normal part of early childhood development. Infants and toddlers use sucking, chewing, and tactile stimulation to calm themselves and explore the world. In most cases these behaviors fade naturally as the child matures. When they persist — particularly past age 4 — they can exert sustained forces on developing bone, teeth, and muscle, leading to orofacial myofunctional disorders that affect breathing, swallowing, speech, and dental alignment.

Habit

Digit sucking

Thumb and finger sucking is the most common oral habit seen in children. While non-nutritive sucking is completely normal in infancy, prolonged digit sucking beyond ages 3–4 can reshape the palate, push upper front teeth forward into an overjet, create an open bite where the front teeth no longer meet, and narrow the upper dental arch — leading to crowding and cross-bites. The sustained inward pressure of the cheeks combined with the outward pressure of the thumb or fingers disrupts the delicate balance of forces that guide normal dental and facial growth. Digit sucking also frequently co-occurs with tongue thrust, as the tongue adapts its resting position to accommodate the digit. Kathleen trained directly under Rose Van Norman — widely considered the leading authority on digit sucking cessation — and uses a positive, child-centered approach that builds the child's own motivation to stop rather than relying on punishment, shame, or physical deterrents.

Habit

Nail biting

Chronic nail biting (onychophagia) is one of the most prevalent oral habits in both children and adults. Beyond the visible damage to the nails and surrounding skin, persistent nail biting places repetitive stress on the front teeth and jaw joints. Over time this can cause chipping, cracking, and abnormal wear of the teeth, contribute to TMJ dysfunction and jaw pain, and introduce bacteria from under the nails into the mouth — increasing the risk of infection. In the context of orofacial myofunctional health, nail biting keeps the jaw in a forward, protruded position and reinforces dysfunctional muscle patterns that interfere with proper oral rest posture. Like other oral habits, nail biting is often tied to stress, anxiety, or boredom, and treatment focuses on building awareness of the behavior, identifying triggers, and developing replacement strategies that support healthy orofacial function.

Habit

Hair twirling

Hair twirling and hair pulling (trichotillomania in its more severe form) may not seem directly related to orofacial health, but these self-soothing behaviors frequently co-occur with oral habits like thumb sucking, lip chewing, and nail biting. They share common neurological roots — all are body-focused repetitive behaviors driven by the same underlying need for sensory input or self-regulation. When hair twirling is accompanied by mouthing or chewing on the hair, it becomes a direct orofacial concern, introducing foreign material into the mouth and reinforcing dysfunctional oral patterns. Even without the mouthing component, persistent hair twirling in a child often signals the same sensory-seeking or anxiety-driven patterns that sustain other oral habits. Addressing the full picture of a child's self-soothing behaviors — rather than targeting one habit in isolation — produces more lasting results and helps the child develop healthier coping strategies overall.

"Children stop these habits when they're ready — our job is to help them get ready. The approach is always positive, never punitive."
