Habits & behaviors

Oral Manifestations

Many common habits — thumb sucking, nail biting, hair twirling — may seem harmless, but when they persist they can have real consequences for orofacial development and function.

Understanding oral habits

Repetitive oral and self-soothing habits are a normal part of early childhood. When they persist — particularly past age 4 — they can reshape developing bone, teeth, and muscle, leading to orofacial myofunctional disorders.

Habit

Digit sucking

Thumb and finger sucking is the most common oral habit in children. While completely normal in infancy, prolonged sucking beyond ages 3–4 disrupts the balance of forces that guide dental and facial growth.

Overjet

The thumb pushes upper front teeth outward, creating protruding "buck teeth."

Open bite

A gap forms between upper and lower front teeth, making it impossible to bite through food cleanly.

Narrowed palate

Sustained pressure narrows the upper arch, contributing to crowding and cross-bites.

Tongue thrust

The tongue adapts its resting position around the digit, often developing a forward thrust pattern that persists even after the habit stops.

Our approach

Kathleen trained directly under Rose Van Norman — the leading authority on digit sucking cessation. Her method is always positive and child-centered, building the child's own motivation to stop rather than relying on shame, punishment, or physical deterrents.

Habit

Nail biting

Chronic nail biting (onychophagia) is one of the most prevalent oral habits in both children and adults. It places repetitive stress on the teeth and jaw — and reinforces dysfunctional muscle patterns.

Chips, cracks, and abnormal wear on the front teeth
TMJ strain and jaw pain from repetitive forward jaw positioning
Bacteria transfer from nails to mouth, increasing infection risk
Reinforces improper oral rest posture — jaw forward, lips apart
Often tied to stress, anxiety, or boredom — treatment addresses triggers

Who it affects

Unlike digit sucking, nail biting commonly persists into adolescence and adulthood. It's estimated to affect 20–30% of the general population.

How we treat it

Treatment focuses on building awareness of the behavior, identifying triggers, and developing replacement strategies that support healthy orofacial function.

Habit

Hair twirling

Hair twirling may not seem related to orofacial health, but it frequently co-occurs with oral habits and shares the same neurological roots — all are body-focused repetitive behaviors driven by a need for sensory input or self-regulation.

The connection to OMDs

Often accompanies thumb sucking, lip chewing, and nail biting
When combined with mouthing or chewing hair, becomes a direct oral concern
Signals the same sensory-seeking patterns that sustain oral habits

Why the whole picture matters

Targeting one habit in isolation often leads to substitution with another
Addressing the full range of self-soothing behaviors produces lasting results
Helps children develop healthier coping strategies overall

"Children stop these habits when they're ready — our job is to help them get ready. The approach is always positive, never punitive."

— Kathleen M. Elder, RDH, COM
