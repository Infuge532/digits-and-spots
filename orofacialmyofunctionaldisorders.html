Understanding the condition

What are Orofacial Myofunctional Disorders?

Orofacial myofunctional disorders (OMDs) are patterns of abnormal muscle posture and movement in and around the mouth and face — affecting how we breathe, speak, chew, and swallow.

The basics

What are Orofacial Myofunctional Disorders?

An orofacial myofunctional disorder (OMD) includes a variety of habit patterns, postures, and functional activities that open the dental bite beyond what's normal — both at rest and during function. The most recognizable signs include thumb sucking, mouth-open resting posture, forward tongue position, and tongue thrusting during speaking and swallowing.

Left untreated, OMDs can disrupt normal dental development in children and cause over-eruption of selected teeth in adults. All babies are born with a low, forward swallowing pattern — a tongue thrust — which is completely normal. With healthy growth and development, the tongue gradually lifts to rest against the palate. When this transition doesn't happen correctly, OMDs can develop.

Types of Orofacial Myofunctional Disorders

Orofacial myofunctional disorders are not a single condition — they encompass a range of dysfunctional patterns in the muscles of the face, mouth, and throat. Each type affects the body differently, but they often overlap and reinforce one another when left untreated.

Tongue posture & function disorders

The tongue rests in a low, forward position instead of against the palate, or thrusts forward during swallowing and speech. Over time this can reshape the dental arches, create an open bite, and destabilize orthodontic results.

Lip seal incompetence

The lips do not close comfortably at rest, often resulting in a chronic open-mouth posture. This leads to mouth breathing, dry mouth, and can contribute to altered facial growth — particularly in children during key developmental years.

Swallowing pattern disorders

An abnormal swallow — sometimes called a reverse or immature swallow — uses the wrong muscle groups to move food and liquid. Instead of the tongue driving upward against the palate, it pushes against or between the teeth, exerting forces that shift dental alignment over time.

Habitual oral behaviors

Persistent thumb or finger sucking, nail biting, cheek chewing, and prolonged pacifier use are habit-based OMDs. These repetitive forces disrupt the balance between the tongue, lips, and cheeks, reshaping the palate and altering how the teeth come together.

Signs to watch for

Symptoms of Orofacial Myofunctional Disorders

OMDs can manifest in many ways — some obvious, others subtle. Below are the most common symptoms that may indicate an underlying orofacial myofunctional disorder.

Mouth breathing

Habitual breathing through the mouth rather than the nose — especially during sleep or at rest. Mouth breathing can lead to a narrowed upper jaw, altered facial growth, dry mouth, and increased risk of dental problems. Children who breathe through their mouths often develop a long, narrow face and may snore at night.

Tongue thrust

A swallowing pattern where the tongue pushes forward or sideways against the teeth rather than pressing up against the palate. Tongue thrust can create an open bite, push teeth out of alignment, and undermine orthodontic treatment. It affects both speech clarity and dental stability.

Speaking disorders

Lisps, slurred speech, and difficulty pronouncing certain sounds — particularly "s," "z," "sh," and "ch" — can stem from improper tongue placement and weak orofacial muscles. These speech patterns often persist despite traditional speech therapy when the underlying muscle dysfunction is not addressed.

Tethered oral tissues

Tongue-ties (ankyloglossia) and lip-ties are restrictions in the connective tissue beneath the tongue or upper lip that limit range of motion. These tethered tissues can prevent the tongue from resting properly against the palate, contribute to breathing and feeding difficulties, and are a common underlying cause of other OMD symptoms.

Breathing disorders

Sleep-disordered breathing, snoring, and upper airway resistance syndrome can be connected to orofacial dysfunction. When the tongue does not maintain proper resting posture against the palate, it can fall back and obstruct the airway during sleep — particularly in children with underdeveloped jaws.

Jaw pain & headaches

Improper oral rest posture and dysfunctional swallowing patterns place excess strain on the jaw joints (TMJ) and surrounding muscles. This can lead to chronic jaw pain, tension headaches, facial discomfort, and clicking or popping in the jaw — symptoms that are often misdiagnosed or treated without addressing the root cause.

Digestive disorders

Improper chewing and swallowing — such as taking overly large bites, not chewing food thoroughly, or washing food down with excessive liquid — can contribute to digestive discomfort, bloating, and other gastrointestinal issues. These eating patterns are often a direct result of orofacial muscle dysfunction.

Take the next step

When to see an Orofacial Myofunctional Therapist

If you or your child are experiencing any of these symptoms, schedule an appointment with a certified Orofacial Myofunctional Therapist to develop a treatment plan. Early identification is advantageous, but adults of all ages can benefit from therapy — it's never too late.

You don't need a referral to schedule a consultation. Many patients come to us after noticing persistent symptoms that haven't responded to other treatments, or after being referred by their orthodontist, dentist, or ENT.
