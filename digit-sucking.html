Digit sucking cessation

Thumb & finger sucking — gentle, proven help.

Digit habits are among the most common and most treatable orofacial concerns. Kathleen trained directly under Rose Van Norman — widely considered the leading expert in digit sucking cessation.

Why it matters

When does sucking become a problem?

Non-nutritive sucking (pacifiers, thumbs, fingers) is completely normal and even beneficial for infants. The habit typically fades naturally between ages 2 and 4. When it persists beyond that window, the sustained pressure on developing bone and teeth can cause real, lasting changes.

Upper front teeth tipping forward

The thumb or finger pushes the upper incisors outward, creating an "overjet" — commonly called buck teeth.

Open bite

A persistent gap forms between the upper and lower front teeth, making it impossible to bite cleanly through food.

Narrowed upper palate

Sustained pressure can narrow the arch over time, contributing to tooth crowding and cross-bites.

Tongue thrust development

Digit habits often co-occur with tongue thrust — and eliminating one without the other reduces the chance of lasting correction.

The approach

Without shame. Without force.

Kathleen's method — developed through training with Rose Van Norman, the recognized authority on digit sucking cessation — focuses on positive reinforcement and building the child's own motivation to stop. Children are always treated as partners in the process, not problems to be fixed.

"Children stop sucking when they're ready — our job is to help them get ready. The approach is always positive, never punitive."

— Kathleen M. Elder, RDH, COM
Age-appropriate education about what the habit is doing to their teeth
Building the child's intrinsic motivation — not external pressure or fear
Awareness strategies, not physical deterrents or appliances
Addressing underlying anxiety or triggers that sustain the habit
Follow-up to ensure tongue posture is corrected alongside the digit habit
Frequently asked questions

What parents ask most

Most children naturally stop between ages 2 and 4. If the habit continues past age 4 — especially once permanent teeth begin to erupt — it's worth a consultation. The sooner it's addressed, the less orthodontic intervention may be needed down the road.
Sometimes — in younger children, the teeth and bone can self-correct once the habit stops, especially before permanent teeth have erupted. Older children may need orthodontic follow-up. Kathleen works closely with your child's orthodontist or dentist to assess what's needed.
This is one of the most common concerns parents bring in. Kathleen's approach starts by building the child's own readiness — not imposing a solution from the outside. Most children, once they understand what the habit is doing, become genuinely motivated to stop on their own terms.
Not automatically. Digit habits and tongue thrust often co-exist, and each can reinforce the other. Kathleen addresses both together — cessation of the digit habit and correction of tongue posture — to ensure the most lasting results.
