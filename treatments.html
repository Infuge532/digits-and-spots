Treatments

Our treatment modalities

At Digits & Spots, Kathleen combines her core expertise in orofacial myofunctional therapy with three complementary modalities — craniosacral therapy, primitive reflex integration, and Buteyko breathing — to address the whole patient, not just the symptoms.

Primary therapy

Orofacial Myofunctional Therapy (OMT)

Orofacial myofunctional therapy is a specialized discipline that re-educates the muscles of the mouth, tongue, and face to restore proper rest posture and function. It is the cornerstone of treatment at Digits & Spots.

OMT addresses the root cause of many dental, speech, and breathing problems by retraining the neuromuscular patterns that control how we breathe, chew, swallow, and hold our tongue and lips at rest. Rather than masking symptoms, OMT corrects the underlying dysfunction — and clinical studies show treatment is 80–90% effective, with results maintained long after therapy ends.

The five goals of OMT
1

Eliminate oral habits

Addressing thumb sucking, lip habits, and other patterns driving the dysfunction — gently and without shame or punishment.

2

Establish correct oral rest position

Training the tongue to rest properly against the palate, and the lips to seal comfortably without strain.

3

Establish proper chewing & swallowing

Building new movement patterns for eating and swallowing that feel natural and become automatic over time.

4

Re-pattern head & neck posture

Addressing related postural patterns in the head and neck that contribute to or result from orofacial dysfunction.

5

Habituation of the new muscle pattern

The new patterns become automatic — and corrections last years after therapy ends with continued awareness and monitoring.

Who benefits from OMT

Children

Re-establish a normal oral environment in which healthy dental eruption can occur — ideally before or alongside orthodontic treatment. Early intervention takes advantage of natural growth and development.

Adults

Normalize oral postures and functions to create stability in the dental arches — including after orthodontic relapse or surgery. It's never too late to retrain these patterns.

Complementary therapy

Craniosacral Therapy (CST)

Craniosacral therapy is a gentle, hands-on treatment that uses light touch to evaluate and release restrictions in the craniosacral system — the membranes and cerebrospinal fluid that surround and protect the brain and spinal cord.

By freeing restrictions in this system, CST improves the functioning of the central nervous system and supports the body's natural ability to self-correct. For orofacial myofunctional patients, craniosacral therapy can help release tension in the jaw, face, and neck that contributes to improper oral posture and function.

How it helps OMT patients

CST addresses restrictions in the bones, membranes, and connective tissues of the head and face that may be limiting tongue mobility, jaw movement, or nasal breathing. Releasing these restrictions can accelerate and enhance OMT outcomes.

What to expect

Sessions involve gentle touch — typically no more than the weight of a nickel — applied to various areas of the body. Patients often report a deep sense of relaxation. CST is safe for all ages, from infants to adults.

Common applications

Jaw pain and TMJ dysfunction, chronic headaches, neck and back tension, post-surgical recovery, tongue-tie release support, and infant feeding difficulties are all areas where CST can provide meaningful relief.

Complementary therapy

Primitive Reflex Integration

Primitive reflexes are automatic movement patterns that develop in utero and during infancy. They play a critical role in early survival and neurological development — helping babies root, suck, swallow, and eventually learn to move their bodies in coordinated ways.

In typical development, these reflexes naturally integrate (become inactive) as the brain matures and higher-level motor skills take over, usually within the first year of life. When primitive reflexes are retained beyond their expected timeframe, they can interfere with motor coordination, sensory processing, and — critically for OMT patients — proper orofacial development and function.

The connection to OMDs

Retained reflexes like the rooting reflex, palmar grasp reflex, and Moro reflex can maintain immature oral motor patterns — keeping the tongue low and forward, promoting mouth breathing, and making it difficult for patients to establish and maintain the new muscle patterns OMT is designed to teach.

How integration works

Kathleen uses targeted movement exercises designed to stimulate and then integrate each retained reflex. These exercises are simple, can be done at home, and progressively train the nervous system to release the immature pattern and allow mature motor control to take over.

Signs of retained reflexes

Poor coordination, difficulty sitting still, sensory sensitivities, bedwetting beyond expected age, motion sickness, difficulty with fine motor tasks, and — in the orofacial realm — persistent tongue thrust, open-mouth posture, and difficulty transitioning to mature swallowing patterns.

Complementary therapy

Buteyko Breathing

The Buteyko breathing method is a clinically validated breathing retraining technique developed by Ukrainian physician Dr. Konstantin Buteyko. It focuses on restoring functional nasal breathing and normalizing breathing volume — two factors that are deeply interconnected with orofacial health.

For OMT patients, habitual mouth breathing is one of the most common and damaging patterns. When the mouth is open and the tongue drops to the floor of the mouth to allow airflow, it disrupts proper tongue posture, alters facial growth, dries out oral tissues, and creates a cycle that reinforces the very dysfunction OMT aims to correct. Buteyko breathing breaks this cycle at its source.

Why nasal breathing matters

Nasal breathing filters, warms, and humidifies air before it reaches the lungs. It produces nitric oxide — a vasodilator that improves oxygen absorption. Most importantly for OMT, nasal breathing allows the tongue to rest in its proper position against the palate, maintaining the dental arch shape and supporting healthy facial development.

What Buteyko teaches

Through a series of breathing exercises, patients learn to breathe gently and exclusively through the nose — during the day, during exercise, and during sleep. The method addresses over-breathing (chronic hyperventilation), which is a common but often unrecognized driver of mouth breathing.

Benefits beyond the mouth

Patients who transition to consistent nasal breathing often experience improved sleep quality, reduced snoring, decreased anxiety, better exercise tolerance, fewer asthma symptoms, and a general sense of calm — in addition to the orofacial benefits that support their OMT program.
