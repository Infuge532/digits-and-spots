Understanding the condition

What is an Orofacial Myofunctional Disorder?

OMDs are patterns of abnormal muscle posture and movement in and around the mouth and face — affecting how we breathe, speak, chew, and swallow.

The basics

An OMD opens the bite beyond its normal range

An orofacial myofunctional disorder (OMD) includes a variety of habit patterns, postures, and functional activities that open the dental bite beyond what's normal — both at rest and during function. The most recognizable signs include thumb sucking, mouth-open resting posture, forward tongue position, and tongue thrusting during speaking and swallowing.

Left untreated, OMDs can disrupt normal dental development in children and cause over-eruption of selected teeth in adults.

How it starts

The mouth-body connection

All babies are born with a low, forward swallowing pattern — a tongue thrust — which is completely normal. With healthy growth and development, the tongue gradually lifts to rest against the palate (the roof of the mouth). When this transition doesn't happen correctly, OMDs can develop.

Improper oral habits

Persistent thumb sucking, pacifier use, nail biting, and other habits can interfere with normal muscle development around the mouth.

Restricted nasal airway

When a child can't breathe through their nose — due to allergies, enlarged adenoids, or structural issues — the tongue repositions itself to aid breathing.

Structural abnormalities

A short lingual frenum (tongue-tie) can restrict tongue movement and prevent it from resting properly against the palate.

Neurological or hereditary factors

Some OMDs have neurological or developmental causes, and hereditary predisposition can play a role in how these patterns develop.

Signs to watch for

Common effects of an untreated OMD

Appearance

Upper lip shortening, creating a "gummy" smile appearance
Lower lip lengthening, appearing "pouty"
Open bite — a gap between upper and lower front teeth
Cross-bite from airway-related tongue repositioning

Function

Messy eating — chewing with lips open, noisy sounds
Taking large bites without chewing fully
Washing food down with large amounts of liquid
Difficulty taking pills
Slurred speech or a persistent lisp

"The good news is that orofacial myofunctional disorders are very treatable — and treatment is 80–90% effective. Early identification is advantageous, but adults of all ages can benefit."

— Kathleen M. Elder, RDH, COM
